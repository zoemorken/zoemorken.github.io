A service design approach to improving the student quarantine process at Purdue.

Spring 2021 (5 months, January–May 2021)

Project Brief

During the Spring 2021 semester, my team worked with Avanade on a project as part of our Experience Studio course at Purdue University. We were tasked to create a digital platform to serve as a source of authoritative guidance for the city of West Lafayette. To do this, we decided to focus our efforts on helping students at Purdue University (located in West Lafayette) navigate the quarantine process confidently.

My Role

I co-led a team of seven UX Design students and worked closely with our sponsors. I was responsible for conducting interviews with stakeholders, synthesizing results from team ideation sessions, and designing the mockups for our finalized concept. As a team lead, I built and executed the sprint schedule to complete milestones as well as facilitated remote team activities.

Project Goals
  1. Explore common problems Purdue students face when seeking answers for individual COVID-19 encounters.
  2. Identify students’ pain points with the current resources available to them – namely Protect Purdue.
  3. Propose new ways to bridge the gap of misunderstanding between students and sought-after virus information.
  4. Deliver a solution that allows the university to reduce confusion / frustration around the quarantine process, ultimately promoting a safer living environment.

Stakeholders

The two main stakeholders we focused on for the majority of the project were students at Purdue and nurse case managers. Though our solution is geared toward helping students navigate quarantine smoothly, it was important to take into account the needs and goals of case managers so that Protect Purdue could improve its operations as a whole. Goals and frustrations for each were primarily informed by our interview findings.

User Story

Note: The story presented below was modeled according to Purdue’s quarantine process during the Spring 2021 semester.

Problems Identified
  • What’s being communicated to students during their first phone call with a case manager. Students like David can be overwhelmed by the amount of information thrown at them, especially when they have just learned that they need to move into quarantine housing.
  • Making the timeline and structure of quarantine known to students.Once moved in, students don’t know what they should be doing to set themselves up for success in their temporary living environment.
  • Improving flow and mode of communication between students and case managers. Students and case managers may not get the answers they need in time when using asynchronous forms of communication such as email. Calling is unideal due to the fact that students must wait to be redirected to their case manager.

How might we get students the information they need when in quarantine?

Solution

Our solution tackles two points in David’s journey: before he moves (transition period) and during his stay at Purdue Village (quarantine period). The concept proposed for the transition period deals primarily with how students receive information from Protect Purdue’s website in a time-sensitive situation. On the other hand, the concept proposed for the quarantine period is a tool that would be seamlessly integrated with a student’s Purdue Career (known as “myPurdue”) account.

Research

We began our project by attempting to identify a specific user group within the Purdue student body. Though we had decided to focus on COVID-19 confusion at Purdue University, we believed that narrowing in on a distinct population (whether that be related to living situation, class standing, etc.) would be beneficial in coming up with a solution that addressed their specific needs. Before deciding on a group, we first wanted to understand the true breadth of problems students face due to COVID. In doing so, our goal was to have a grasp on what issues exist on campus before narrowing our focus.

Surveys and Interviews

We began our research with a survey to send out to the general student population. The goal of using a survey was to identify patterns in problems that various student groups across campus face. Respondents that were interested in participating in a follow-up interview were contacted to share more about their personal experiences or anecdotes regarding COVID-related issues at Purdue.

We created our survey using Qualtrics and sent it out to various group chats, Discord servers, and the Purdue subreddit. After leaving it open for one week, our team received a total of 146 responses. Some questions that yielded interesting insights were:

  • How safe do you feel at Purdue in regards to COVID-19?
  • Do you feel like Purdue’s guidelines have been effective in protecting the school?
  • Have you experienced any confusion about the COVID-19 protocol while at Purdue?
Findings:
  • Most students experience confusion on when to quarantine due to second or third hand exposure. Since there is no clear-cut definition of second or third hand exposure, students are unsure which category they fall into and what steps to take next.
  • Protect Purdue fails to adequately update students on policy changes. Protect Purdue tries to update students through emails or website updates, but this does not motivate students to read policy changes in depth.
  • Students don’t know specific regulations for different social settings (e.g. dining courts, dorm rooms, private study rooms, etc.). Students are confused on which guidelines apply in different settings and if there are exceptions.
  • There is no direct correlation between where students are living on campus, and their general confusion with the Protect Purdue guidelines. Regardless of their living situation, students were confused on the same issues.

Based on our survey findings, we were surprised to discover that there was no correlation between a student’s living arrangement and the type of confusion they experience with Protect Purdue guidelines and procedures. Our follow-up interviews demonstrated that there were many opportunities for improvement within the quarantine and isolation process, the greatest being students didn’t know when to quarantine. Because of this, we decided to investigate the process of quarantining at Purdue.

With a general direction to follow, our team wanted to further understand what the quarantine process at Purdue University looks like. We decided to conduct interviews with the Purdue student body in order to get a better picture of the firsthand experience of going through quarantine. Following this, we conducted interviews with members of the Protect Purdue Implementation Team to get the perspective of the people making decisions on behalf of the university. By comparing these experiences with those of students, we aimed to pinpoint areas of disconnect between students and staff.

Student Interviews

We recruited interviewees through the Purdue User Experience Slack workspace, contacting peers that had direct experience going through Purdue’s quarantine procedure. We interviewed a total of 14 students.

Student Findings:
  • Wanted someone to contact when they faced confusion in quarantine. Some students were not given any contact information for their case manager, meaning they were unable to get their questions answered in a timely manner.
  • Did not like having to wait to be connected to a case manager through the PPHC number. When students call the hotline for answers, they are usually put on hold and/or redirected multiple times until someone can provide an answer.
  • Did not find case managers very helpful. Some students reported not hearing from their case manager beyond the initial and final call.
Protect Purdue Interviews

Our interview goals were to learn more about the roles and workflows of a member, determine who the stakeholders are for when a student tests positive, and learn what the are the most commonly reported issues for students.

Participants:
  • Protect Purdue Implementation Team Leader, Chief Innovation and Collaboration Officer
  • Health Management and Surveillance Team Leader
  • Head of Protect Purdue Ambassadors
  • Senior Communications Strategist
  • Nurse Case Manager Supervisor

Some key questions we asked participants were:

  • What student problems do you / your team most frequently run across?
  • Please explain the avenues of communication between your team and the students.
  • Are there any areas where you think the quarantine procedure might be breaking down? If so, what can be improved?
  • What kinds of COVID-19 student affairs do you deal with at work?
Protect Purdue Findings:
  • Case managers decide the student’s situation on a case by case basis. There is no true formula for how the decisions are made.
  • If a student tests negative, they are only contacted by a case manager depending on their situation. Deciding factors include if the student has any recognized COVID symptoms and their level of contact with a person who has tested positive.
  • Case managers are supposed to call students every day while in quarantine.
  • If a student is quarantined in Purdue Village, they are checked on by the PUFD twice daily.

We learned students will seek answers for their Protect Purdue-specific questions on the Protect Purdue website. However, they struggle to find the information they need, so they call the Protect Purdue hotline and are frequently redirected or on hold for a while. Additionally, students in quarantine said that they were in a constant state of confusion or could not get in touch with their case manager.

On the Protect Purdue side, case managers are supposed to be calling quarantined students daily but don’t always do so. Those who try to get into contact with students struggle to do so because students often do not answer phone calls or check emails. The communication between the two stakeholders needs to be improved to relieve frustrations on both ends.

Scoping

Due to the vast amount of data our team had, it was difficult for our team to figure out what problem we wanted to prioritize and solve. We expressed this issue to our sponsors at Avanade and they facilitated a workshop to help us pick a problem to solve.

Our sponsors set up a workshop to complete in Mural. We met with them over two Zoom calls to walk through each of the activities on the board.

Activity 1: Thorn and Bud

We began our workshop by consolidating the data we gathered from interviews, surveys, and other research. The goal of this activity was to visualize the breadth of problems and opportunities. We separated these data points into pain points and opportunities.

Activity 2: Affinity Clustering

After gathering these data points into one place, we grouped sticky notes that shared a common theme. Then, we drew circles around each group and labeled them with overarching themes or issues.

Activity 3: Statement Starters

We then took the pain points and opportunities that we defined in the Affinity Clustering into our Statement Starters activity. With statement starters we wanted to fill in the blanks to statements like "How might we...?"

The goal was to frame problems as questions and encourage generative thinking. By reviewing the pain points on blue sticky notes, our team was able to come up with around 50 questions to combat the negatives of COVID-19 on Purdue’s campus.

Activity 4: Importance Ranking

We ranked the 10 best statements we thought captured an important issue from the previous activity from least to most important. After individually ranking each problem, we came together to discuss our opinions and discern trends which were consistently ranked high across all team members.

Finalized Scope

To reduce confusion when quarantining at Purdue Village by improving communication between students and case management.

Sketching

With research concluded, our team formally began sketching to see how each team member envisioned the solution to our scope. We completed several rounds of sketching. Our goals were twofold: to come to a consensus on the proposed concept and test the desirability of our solution with students. In doing so, we were able to quickly make iterations based on feedback.

For our first round, our team sketched unconstrained to encourage creative and out-of-the-box thinking. We came together in person for the first time to discuss our sketches and determine what features we liked or disliked.

To prepare for the second round, we had a discussion to determine which features should be included and prioritized. The way features were deemed “important” was based on our interview findings; in other words, we made sure each idea was backed up by research. If there was no solid rationale, we saved that idea for later.

Out of the concepts we came up with, the components we wanted to bring to the forefront of our design was a progress bar, a COVID-19 forum for students to answer each other’s questions while in quarantine, and a live chat with students and their assigned case manager.

Decisions:
  • Provided a calendar to help break down the progress bar into further detail. This was to let students know what to expect each day (e.g. a phone call with their case manager, their second COVID test).
  • Integrated our solution into myPurdue so students do not need to learn and use an additional platform for school.
  • Prioritized design of a desktop layout rather than mobile app. With the integration of our solution into myPurdue and the fact that students would most likely be in their rooms, we decided to sketch desktop views of our solution.

Sketch Concept Testing

With these sketches completed, we moved forward with concept testing to see if our solution would prove useful for students going through quarantine. Our goal for testing with sketches was to be able to make iterations earlier in the process and minimize mistakes in the experience when creating higher fidelity mockups.

Findings:
  • Participants liked seeing a progress bar but would prefer days left instead of percentage of how much quarantine they completed.
  • Most believed that the live chat would help reduce the amount of phone calls Protect Purdue has to handle.
  • The packing list should be more prominently displayed. Participants stressed the need for a packing list, as there were items they wished they brought with them when moving into temporary housing.
  • Most participants did not find the forum useful. Testers believed that students would not make use of it. Additionally, they explained if they had an option to talk to a case manager, they would rather just message their case manager through the live chat.

With our first round of concept testing completed, we removed the forum. Most importantly, we were able to verify our design being something students in Purdue Village could actually see themselves using.

Wireframing

With concept testing completed, our team created wireframes in order to help visualize the layout of our agreed-upon features. This informed the final medium-fidelity design, which was put under another round of testing to gain more insight on the usability of our solution.

Wireframe Concept Testing

We conducted a second round of concept testing to ensure our design was still useful to students, especially since the design was higher in fidelity and the forum was removed.

Findings:
  • The calendar and live chat were well received. Students explained that when quarantined, days tend to blend with each other. Visual indications or trackers of their progress was helpful in keeping track of time.
  • The live chat was something students wished they had in quarantine. One participant noted that having a written form of communication was better so that they could refer back to past conversations if needed.
  • Consolidating and organizing relevant resources was helpful because students do not need to hunt for information anymore.

From this testing we decided to reorganize the resources page to incorporate a hierarchy of the most to least used resources.

Next Steps

If we were to continue our project, we would continue building out high-fidelity mockups based on our feedback. Another possibility would be to conduct usability testing to ensure the layout of the solution is easy to understand. As always, more research would ensure that our design meets the evolving needs of students as much as possible. Some ideas include:

  • Making an interactive Minimum Viable Product (MVP).An interactive MVP would allow testers and users to clearly see how our design could be incorporated into their experience.
  • Conducting contextual inquiry research. If it wasn’t risky, our team could conduct contextual inquiries by going to Purdue Village and interviewing individuals currently in quarantine. We would be able to learn firsthand what the experience looks like while students are currently in quarantine.
  • Doing a diary study. A diary study is a method that is “used to collect qualitative data about user behaviors, activities, and experiences over time. In a diary study, data is self-reported by participants longitudinally” (Salazar, 2016). Considering that students are in temporary housing for two weeks, we could ask students coming in whether they would like to opt into a study documenting their activities and feelings.
  • Interview students in a wide variety of majors, especially those who frequently go to class in person. Students with in-person classes could have an entirely different issue / perspective we could design for.

From this testing we decided to reorganize the resources page to incorporate a hierarchy of the most to least used resources.

Reflection

Overall, our team had a great semester. We were able to work with supportive sponsors who guided us through a tough problem space. We also developed a better appreciation for research thanks to how research-intensive this project was. Though we were unable to reach higher fidelity work, our team gathered a lot of invaluable research and testing in order to make the UI a solo project that will allow for individual practice.

Key Terms

  • Mural – Remote whiteboarding tool intended for visual collaboration within teams.
  • Nurse Case Manager – The first point of contact when the Protect Purdue Health Center (PPHC) determines a student has or is at risk of contracting the virus. Students are assigned a case manager who decides on an appropriate course of care and monitors the student’s health regularly when in quarantine or isolation.
  • Protect Purdue – Purdue University’s comprehensive strategy to keep the campus and community safe by limiting the spread of COVID-19. More information can be found here.
  • Protect Purdue Implementation Team – The team in charge of developing and enacting the Protect Purdue Plan.
  • Purdue Career Account (myPurdue) – A portal that allows students to manage their financial aid, academics and more during their time at Purdue.
