Zoë Morken Work About Resume

Meal Train

A platform redesigned to support cancer patients through food and conversation.

Spring 2021 (5 months, January–May 2020)

Project Brief

During the Spring 2020 semester, I worked on my capstone project as part of my Learning Studio course at Purdue University. I chose to focus my efforts on redesigning Meal Train to support cancer patients and their caregivers through the treatment process. As the sole UX Designer on this project, I determined primary stakeholder needs and motivations through various research methods as well as designed an end-to-end solution that would facilitate trust between those involved.

Problem

When middle-aged cancer patients are in the recovery phase of treatment, their caregivers (typically a partner, family member, or close friend) often have many responsibilities that can leave them overwhelmed. One of these primary responsibilities is ensuring that the patient is being fed nutrition-dense, healthy food.

Meal registry services like Meal Train allow cancer patients and their caregivers to receive the support they need. By sending out invites to friends and family over email or social media, Meal Train raises awareness of the situation and facilitates volunteerism. Though the platform is able to help people who have already gone through a life-changing event (e.g. a birth or death in the family, recovery from a medical procedure), it does not afford the flexibility required for cancer patients with needs that evolve over time. Therefore, I wanted to alter Meal Train in a way that better suited the unique physical and emotional challenges that cancer patients face.

To sum:

Purpose: to alleviate the burden of thinking about / performing mundane, everyday tasks for cancer patients undergoing treatment and their caregivers.

Intention: to redesign the Meal Train platform so that cancer patients undergoing treatment and their caregivers can receive support through meal assistance and in-person visits.

Stakeholders

There are three, primary stakeholders I considered when redesigning Meal Train:

Cancer Patient

The person in need and the primary recipient of the meal train.

Caregiver

The person supporting the cancer patient and the primary organizer of the meal train. The term does not apply to a professional, paid caregiver.

Broader Support Network

The people who are connected to either the patient or the caregiver in some way and are willing to assist if needed; volunteers contributing their time and energy to the meal train.

User Story

Research

To familiarize myself with each stakeholders' needs, I read articles and peer-reviewed journals on what cancer looks like and the role of diet in recovery. I then conducted interviews to hear more about individual experiences living with cancer or supporting someone undergoing treatment.

The guiding topic for my secondary research was food handling and nutrition requirements for different types of cancer patients. Because patients typically follow strict eating guidelines set by medical professionals to improve their chances of survival, this is an important area I aimed to emphasize when redesigning Meal Train. The overarching question I wanted to answer within this body of knowledge was “How does nutrition impact the health of cancer patients undergoing treatment?”

In general, patients need extra calories to keep their energy levels up during treatment. This can be tricky due to side effects that can prevent patients from wanting to eat. When setting preferences in a redesigned version of Meal Train, it is important that organizers can easily customize dietary requirements. Ways the platform can be tailored towards the needs of patients and caregivers could look like asking what the type of cancer the patient has and the side effects they’re experiencing.

Interviews

I interviewed two cancer survivors and three people with experience caring for someone with cancer. For cancer survivors, my intent was to learn more about their experience undergoing treatment and the type of support they received during this time. Likewise, I wanted to hear from caregivers and supporters what their role entailed. Two of the three interviewees were friends and family of the patient, while one was a former nurse practitioner who worked in oncology. The interviews were conducted through Zoom and Google Meet, lasting from 30 minutes to an hour.

Cancer Patient Findings
  • Having a dedicated caregiver and people coming in to visit (for short periods of time) is incredibly helpful, physically and emotionally.
  • It was best when people gave without telling the cancer patient to reach out if they needed help. Both participants initially felt uncomfortable asking for help, but after knowing people wanted to offer their services, they began to ask for favors. However, unexpected acts of kindness from their support system took stress away from reaching out in times of need.
    • "People would just bring food and I was very grateful. Thank you very much! I didn’t know I should be saying yes. Don't ask, just take it. We were very grateful when people showed up on the doorstep with food."
    "So I just think there are so many things that can be overwhelming, but don’t be afraid to ask for the help that you need because there’s people in your life who care and there’s also strangers who’ve been touched by cancer in some way..."
  • The dietary needs and preferences for a patient are likely to change over time. For the patient undergoing chemotherapy, her taste buds altered greatly. Sweet foods, such as cookies or fruit, tasted bitter to her and many drinks felt dehydrating. The food she requested from friends depended mostly on what sounded palatable after each infusion.
Caregiver / Supporter Findings
  • It’s important to recognize that the cancer patient is sick, but that they are still themselves. According to one supporter, people can be frightened by a loved one having cancer and may adopt an “overly sympathetic approach” when visiting the patient.
    • “People’s reaction to cancer is very individual. Each person reacts differently to the fact that someone they care about or love has cancer. Some people can't see the person because all they can think about is that the person has cancer. I’ve heard cancer patient after cancer patient say that they just want to be looked at and treated as an individual, as themselves and they don’t want to necessarily be treated as a cancer patient.”
  • The best you can do for someone undergoing treatment is to accept them as they are. The emotional toll that very often comes with news of cancer can be overwhelming. As a caregiver, it’s important to give them the support needed by not allowing your own emotions to get in the way, but to listen and soothe.
    • “[Be] unafraid to just be with someone in their grief or their anger. Listen, be an exceptionally good listener, just listen and [accept] whatever you see or hear because they really are grappling with something that’s almost beyond comprehension to them.”
  • When caring for a patient, the meal experience should be well-thought through. Since patients are in a delicate physical and mental state, caregivers should prepare the patient’s meal with intention and make sure to take care of anything hindering the patient from eating. Suggestions include preparing food (e.g. cut up ahead of time, served at the right temperature), keeping fluid readily accessible, and asking if they need to use the bathroom before and after eating.

Reimagining the Space

Though I had previously generated design concepts that would build off the current platform, I realized that the changes I had made would potentially get lost in the bigger picture. To reframe the space and better apply research insights, I decided to reimagine the platform as an application that would connect cancer patients to people offering support through meal assistance and conversation. Adding the conversation piece was the most significant change I made to the platform. This was done primarily to address the patient’s need for emotional support, especially in a situation like Fiona’s.

Below are the pages from my sketchbook that capture my thought process. After listing out important takeaways, I categorized cancer patients into two situations: one where they have a dedicated caregiver (e.g. a spouse) helping them, and one with inconsistent support. After thinking through these situations where the organizer of the meal train could either be the caregiver or the patient themselves, I came to the conclusion that Meal Train should have a social aspect involved. Instead of just delivering food, supporters can prepare and share a meal with the patient and caregiver. This idea was formed on the finding that patients were able to make it through their journey knowing that people cared for their wellbeing. I also wanted to make sure the patient living alone was supported while eating. Having a visitor stop by could make it easier to observe whether the patient was getting the nutrition they need.

Sketching and Ideation

I approached the redesign by drafting Fiona’s story in order to better determine what screens were needed for the stakeholders involved. Following this, I created screens for the app starting from sketches and gradually worked my way toward mid-fidelity mockups. After each round of sketching or wireframing, I tested with participants to understand whether stakeholders would want or need the proposed services of Meal Train. I also wanted to check whether what I was creating could fit within the current ecosystem.

The purpose of sketching was to translate my research outcomes into a realistic solution for my stakeholders. Before moving on to mockups, my goal was to lay the groundwork and determine what information and functionality is needed for different users interacting with the app.

Sketch Concept Testing

For this round, I tested my sketches with two participants whom I had interviewed previously. To get feedback on my sketches, I imported them to Figma and arranged them in a way that aligned chronologically with the way users would interact with the app. During our call, I set the stage for my app by introducing participants to the user story on Fiona. After doing so, I shared my screen and presented the sketches in sections, starting with patient screens and ending with participant visiting screens.

Findings:
  • Food preferences may change spontaneously during treatment. One participant mentioned that her tastes altered dramatically each time she received an infusion. What once tasted good to her several weeks ago could hardly be stomached the next time around.
  • There was confusion regarding what updates were for. The first participant thought updates were food-specific, while the other thought this would be a way to chat one-on-one with a volunteer.
  • The prayer box is a good alternative for patients that can’t meet or are limited as far as visits go due to their weakened immune system. Some patients may be in such a vulnerable state that they should not be meeting others at all.
Changes

From testing results, I took away three points when making changes to my sketches:

  • Make it easy for patients to update volunteers on what they would like to eat.
  • Consider calendar timing within the app (patient infusions, surgeries, etc. and how they’re feeling afterwards).
  • Simplify the concept for patients signing up on their own.

Wireframe Concept Testing

I followed the same process as I did for the first round of concept testing.

Findings:
  • There was confusion regarding what updates were for. Participants were unsure as to who was updating who and where the direction of communication was going.
  • Volunteers, especially if they’re older and not as “tech savvy,” may struggle using the app. One participant said that though she would be able to figure it out, she knew of several people who would find the app tricky to navigate. This is a problem because older people who have the time and desire to help may be discouraged to do so.

The most important takeaway was that my app wouldn’t work for the intended stakeholders. The functionality was too much all at once, and this proved to be a major hindrance for my testing participants.

“The more things that we could handle as volunteers would be good and then they don’t have to think or worry about it. This reminds me of when I had COVID during Christmas and New Years weeks and could barely think straight. I suspect someone receiving chemo would feel the same way. There is no way in the world that I would’ve wanted access to these details.”

Refocusing on the People

After both rounds of testing, it was apparent that I had lost sight of who would be interacting with the redesigned Meal Train: organizers and visitors most likely middle-aged or older. Though I had mapped out a clear vision on how users could engage with the app, I had missed the mark on whether my stakeholders would find the app intuitive and easy to use. I realized that I needed to define who the organizer of the app would be as well as simplify the concept overall.

Iterations

For Fiona

In the user story scenario I gave participants during testing, I wrote it so that Fiona would be both the recipient and the organizer: no Ruth involved. From concept testing, I quickly understood why the current platform assumes that the recipient cannot be the organizer: namely, the physical and emotional burden of whatever the recipient is going through will overpower any desire to advocate for themselves. Because of this, I decided to create a way for patients to stay in the loop without being involved in the intricacies of the meal train itself. Therefore, the recipient needed their own view of Meal Train, separate from the organizer and visitors.

Fiona only sees three tabs: Today, Upcoming, and Prayers. That way, she is reminded of who’s showing up from day to day, anticipates who’s coming in the next couple of weeks, and access prayers. She can chat with Ruth at any time by clicking the icon on the top right. Functionality is stripped down to its essence so that Fiona can participate without having to organize it herself.

For Ruth

Major changes to the organizer role:

  • Only Ruth can post status updates on Fiona.
  • Ruth is the only stakeholder with the ability to message Fiona and visitors; she is the intermediary between them.
  • Ruth cannot see prayers that visitors send. Prayers can be highly personal, and if the organizer is not close with the recipient, the recipient nor the visitors may not want the organizer seeing their notes.
  • Settings are for the meal train, not the organizer. In a future iteration, Ruth could manage multiple trains from this page.
For the Community

Feedback I received from testing that I hadn’t considered before was difficulty using the app on the visitor side. Before, visitors could expect to see a similar layout as the organizer. My testing participants didn’t believe this to be necessary and thought that the organizer would have to explain the screens to visitors as they onboard onto the app. Though I assumed that there would be a tutorial for all stakeholders when they first see their respective layout, the visitor screens were still too difficult to navigate for some within the age bracket I was targeting. I slimmed down the functionality to include only what visitors needed in order to successfully help Fiona out.
